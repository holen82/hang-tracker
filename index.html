L1
Passive Dead Hang
Foundation — grip, shoulder stability, body weight tolerance

The passive dead hang is the starting point. You hang from the bar completely relaxed — arms straight, shoulders loose, feet off the ground. Despite feeling simple, it builds grip strength, decompresses the spine, and begins conditioning your tendons and connective tissue.

How to do it: Grip the bar with both hands slightly wider than shoulder-width, palms facing away. Let your body hang with arms fully extended. Do not brace or tense — the point is passive loading.

↑ Hands shoulder-width or slightly wider
↑ Arms fully extended, elbows soft (not locked)
↑ Shoulders relaxed and raised toward ears
↑ Body still — no swinging or kicking

What you should feel: A comfortable stretch across the lats, shoulders, and upper back. Mild grip fatigue is normal. Nothing sharp or painful.

GtG rule: Stop well before your grip gives out. Each hang should feel easy — if you're shaking, you went too long.
L2
Active Hang
Activation — engage the lats, build pulling readiness

The active hang looks identical to the passive hang from the outside, but involves deliberately engaging your shoulder muscles. By depressing and retracting your scapulae (shoulder blades), you activate the lats — the primary muscles used in a pull-up. This is the single most important step most beginners skip.

How to do it: Start in a passive hang. Then, without bending your elbows, pull your shoulders down and back — think "away from your ears" and "together behind you." Your chest will rise slightly and your body will lift a centimetre or two. Hold this engaged position for the duration.

↑ Start from a relaxed passive hang
↑ Pull shoulder blades DOWN (away from ears)
↑ Pull shoulder blades BACK (together behind you)
↑ Arms stay straight throughout — no elbow bend
↑ Feel your chest open and rise slightly

What you should feel: A clear activation in your upper back and lats. This is genuinely harder than it looks — your target time resets lower because it's a different, more demanding exercise.

Common mistake: Bending the elbows. The scapular movement should come entirely from the shoulder girdle, not the arms.
L3
Scapular Shrugs
Strength — build the first movement of a pull-up

Scapular shrugs are the first true pulling movement on your way to a pull-up. Starting from an active hang, you perform a small but deliberate downward pull of the shoulder blades — elevating your body slightly without bending the elbows. This strengthens the lower trapezius and the initial phase of the pull-up in isolation.

How to do it: Get into a solid active hang (L2 position). From there, drive your shoulder blades further down and squeeze them together to raise your body a few centimetres. Hold the top for 1–2 seconds, then slowly lower back to the active hang. That's one rep. The app counts reps — use the + / − buttons after each one.

↑ Begin in active hang — shoulders already engaged
↑ Drive scapulae DOWN and IN to rise slightly
↑ Hold the top for 1–2 seconds
↑ Lower slowly and with control
↑ Elbows remain straight throughout
↑ Reset fully to active hang between reps

What you should feel: A strong contraction in the mid-back and lower traps. The movement range is small — only a few centimetres — but the muscular effort is significant.

Quality over quantity: One perfect shrug with a 2-second hold is worth more than five sloppy ones. If your elbows start bending, stop and rest.
